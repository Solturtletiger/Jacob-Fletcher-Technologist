EVERFLOW

Smart Self-Watering Plant Pot with Arduino Automation

Project Overview

Everflow is a smart, automated self-watering plant pot powered by an Arduino microcontroller. It automatically detects when a plant needs water and waters it without human intervention, keeping plants healthy while reducing water waste.

The system uses a soil moisture sensor buried in the soil basin. When readings fall below a programmed threshold, an Arduino Uno triggers a 5V water pump in a separate water basin. Water is funnelled up through a hidden PVC tube to a tiered "waterfall" feature, cascading down into the soil. LED lights provide visual feedback—green when watering, red when moisture is fine.

Technologies & Tools

Arduino Uno
Acts as the brain, reading sensor data and controlling the pump and LED feedback.
 Soil Moisture Sensor
Continuously monitors moisture levels in the soil basin.
 5V Water Pump
Hidden in the water basin, activated when soil is too dry.
 Embedded Software
Custom Arduino code for threshold comparison and pump/LED control.
 Industrial Design
Tiered cascading shape inspired by natural rock formations; waterfall also provides relaxing ambient sound.
 User-Centred Design
Two design directions explored: Everflow (main) and Crybaby (playful alternative for younger users).
 Commercial Viability
Manufacturing cost £11.93/unit at 10k scale; £30 retail price for strong margin.

Design Concepts

Everflow (Main Concept): Features a tiered, cascading shape inspired by natural rock formations. The waterfall feature was included not only for watering but because the sound of flowing water is psychologically proven to provide relaxing background noise and improve mental health.

Crybaby (Alternative Concept): A playful design aimed at younger users. The pot takes the form of a cute, crying character—children may forget to water plants, so this self-watering "creature" keeps the plant alive by "crying tears of life" into the soil, engaging kids through a fun aesthetic.

Additional Explorations

The team explored sustainability through a light-tracking solar panel to power the pot renewably. Due to space constraints inside the pot and the 2-month project timeline, the solar tracker was excluded from the final prototype to keep the design simple and user-friendly. The project successfully combines mechanical engineering, embedded software, user-centred industrial design, and business modelling into a smart-home gardening accessory.
